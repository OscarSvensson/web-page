Introduktion

This page is about the pursuit of living off the land and using the resources found in nature to provide for oneself. Humans have had a close relationship with the land for thousands of years, only to lose that connection over the span of a few generations. My wish is to find my way back. At least in some form or capacity.

Safety

The stuff I describe is how I do things. That does not always mean that it will work for you or your situation. I am not an expert, and you might not be either. Use your own judgement, and do the research before attempting any project. Activities such as identifying plants and mushrooms for eating, felling trees, working with sharp tools or lighting fires are all safe when done right. But can be hazardous or even lethal in some cases if you are uncareful.

Rights of usage

I will describe what you can do with the stuff you find in nature, and not whether you are allowed to do so. That will depend on where in the world you are, who owns the land, and other stuff. I am fortunate enough to own land, but there is some stuff that is available for anyone to forage in most countries.

Laundry detergent from Horse chestnut

Conkers from Aesculus hippocastanum, horse chestnut, can be used as a laundry detergent. The conkers contain some stuf that make them ineddible, but are also rich in saponins. Saponins are a natural soapy substance that help disolve grease.

Harvesting

When conkers drop to the ground in autumn. They are easy to both find and identify, once you have found a tree. Each tree also drop huge amounts of conkers, wich makes it easy to harvest large amounts.

Processing and storage

You can use or process them in a number of ways, but the way that I personally find easiest is to quarter and dry them. Simply chop each one into four pieces and spread them out and leave them in a warm dry place for a few days.
Once dry they store in a glass jar for years, provided that you keep them away from moisture.
I have read of methods where you grind or chop the conkers to a powder, which should help the saponins leech out easier. I do however find that the quarter size pieces do the job just fine, and if I can save some time and effort and just use the larger pieces, that is the way to go for me.
Some sources will tell you to remove the brown shell when washing white laundry, as this part is said to cause miscolorings on the laundry. I have not seen this happen myself, but you might be careful with this, and avoid washing any heirloom textiles this way.

Use in washing machine

Take 6-8 dried or fresh quarters and tie them in a sock. throw in the sock together with the rest of the laundry, and run the same program you would otherwise have used.
